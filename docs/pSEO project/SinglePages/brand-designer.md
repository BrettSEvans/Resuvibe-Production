## FAQ

### Q1: Why are these specific keywords critical for my Brand Designer resume?

**Short answer:** Hiring managers and ATS systems screen resumes first by **skill match**. If the keywords they're looking for aren't on your resume — even if you have the experience — your application gets filtered out before a human ever reads it.

**The mechanism:** When a company posts a job for a Brand Designer, their ATS is configured to look for specific terms that match the role. The ATS scans your resume for exact or near-exact matches. If you list skills only in conversational language ("I built systems with databases"), but the job description says specific technical terms, the parser may not connect the dots.

**Why it matters:** Studies show that **40–70% of qualified resumes are rejected by ATS systems** before recruiters see them, simply because keywords weren't present or clearly stated. This isn't a bias — it's a scaling problem. Recruiters can't manually review thousands of applications, so they rely on systems to surface the most relevant matches first.

**What you should do:** Mirror the job description's exact language in your skills section, professional summary, and bullet points. This is **matching your experience to the employer's language**, which is exactly what a Brand Designer hiring manager expects.

### Q2: How do I actually know if my resume is ATS-optimized?

**The honest answer:** You probably don't, and that's normal. Most job seekers can't see inside an ATS system to verify what made it through. But you can audit your resume against the job description:

1. **Download the job posting** and open it alongside your resume
2. **Highlight the skills and keywords** that appear in the job description (typically in the "Required Qualifications" and "Nice to Have" sections)
3. **Search your resume** for exact matches. If a keyword appears in the job description but not on your resume, that's a gap.
4. **Check your formatting:** ATS systems struggle with headers, sidebars, graphics, and unusual fonts. Stick to a clean, single-column layout with standard fonts (Arial, Calibri, Times New Roman)
5. **Test it:** Some ATS systems publish "resume scoring" tools that give you instant feedback on keyword match and formatting compliance

**The limitation:** Even with these steps, you're still guessing. You're optimizing for *possible* keywords, not the actual ATS configuration that company is using.

**Better approach:** Focus on **honest, keyword-rich resume language** that accurately represents your experience. Use the job description as a guide, but don't fabricate skills.

### Q3: What's the actual difference between a passive bullet and a quantified one? Does it really matter?

**Yes, it matters — but not just for ATS.**

A passive bullet like *"Responsible for software development tasks"* tells the hiring manager almost nothing. A quantified bullet like *"Architected and deployed a microservices platform (Python, Kubernetes) serving 5M+ daily requests, reducing server latency by 40%"* does several things:

1. **Shows scale** — The hiring manager immediately understands the scope of your work
2. **Proves impact** — You didn't just do the work; you measurably moved a needle
3. **Demonstrates your toolkit** — Specific technologies and business outcomes you've delivered
4. **Makes you memorable** — Numbers stick in people's minds way better than job duties

**Does ATS care?** Not directly. But once your resume lands on a human's desk, they spend **6–7 seconds** on the first pass. A vague bullet gets skipped. A strong, quantified bullet gets read and remembered.

**The reframe:** Stop thinking of bullets as "job descriptions" and start thinking of them as **proof of value**. For each bullet, ask: "What's the evidence that I'm good at this?"
